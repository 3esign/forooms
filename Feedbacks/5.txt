This is an ambitious and technically feasible vision. However, the difference between a research prototype and a platform that can actually scale to thousands of digital twins is almost entirely in the data architecture, not the rendering. Most "Minecraft digital twin" projects fail because they save millions of voxels instead of saving the procedural recipe that generates them.

For Foroom, I would strongly recommend adopting the same philosophy used by Minecraft, modern GIS systems, Cesium, Google Maps, Unreal World Partition and procedural game engines:

Never store the world. Store only the instructions needed to recreate the world plus the user modifications.

That single principle should drive the entire architecture.

Bounding Limits

Recommended maximum initial forum size:

2 × 2 km at 2 m voxel resolution (about 1 million ground cells before extrusion), suitable for smooth browser performance.
Absolute hard limit for version 1: 4 × 4 km, with asynchronous generation and stricter level-of-detail management.

Larger regions should eventually be partitioned into streamed regions instead of generated as one continuous world.

Long-term vision

The most valuable interpretation of Foroom is not as a "Minecraft clone," but as a procedural civic operating system. OpenStreetMap provides a reproducible geometric foundation, deterministic procedural generation reconstructs that foundation on demand, and only human contributions—block edits, discussions, proposals, votes, simulations, and governance metadata—are stored permanently. This architecture minimizes storage and bandwidth while enabling virtually unlimited numbers of persistent forums. Each Foroom becomes a lightweight, shareable, living digital twin that evolves through citizen participation rather than through static datasets, allowing communities to collaboratively explore, redesign, debate, and simulate the future of real urban environments in an accessible voxel-based metaverse.
