## 1. Executive summary

Foroom sits at the intersection of four research traditions that have historically stayed fairly separate:

1. **Participatory planning theory** — decades of work on *what counts as real citizen power* versus symbolic consultation (Arnstein's Ladder; communicative/collaborative planning).
2. **Urban digital twins & complexity science** — how to represent a city computationally without pretending it is predictable in the way a bridge or engine is.
3. **Deliberative technology / computational social choice** — the emerging generation of AI-assisted tools (Pol.is, vTaiwan, DeepMind's Habermas Machine, participatory-budgeting algorithms) that scale consensus-finding beyond what human facilitators can do.
4. **Game design & spatial-cognition science** — why Minecraft-style tools (UN-Habitat's *Block by Block* programme) work as participation devices, and what space syntax and procedural-generation research tells us about representing cities as voxel grids without losing legibility.

The strongest version of Foroom treats these not as decoration but as **load-bearing design constraints**: the role system (Citizen → Participant → Builder → Admin) should map onto Arnstein's ladder; the three layers (Council / Playground / Simulation) should map onto distinct epistemic functions (accurate record vs. free exploration vs. forecasting); and the AI Protector should be built on the deliberative-AI literature (Pol.is/Habermas Machine) rather than invented from scratch as a generic chatbot moderator.

The report below expands each pillar, cites the relevant precedent systems and papers, flags known failure modes documented in the literature (tokenism, dark patterns in gamification, "digital twin" overclaiming), and ends with an integration roadmap.

## 2. Pillar I — Theories of citizen participation (what makes participation *real*)

### 2.1 Arnstein's Ladder of Citizen Participation

Sherry Arnstein's 1969 model remains the reference framework planners use to judge whether an engagement process is genuine or performative. It describes eight rungs grouped into three bands: **non-participation** (manipulation, therapy), **tokenism** (informing, consultation, placation), and **citizen power** (partnership, delegated power, citizen control). The framework's core warning is that most "participatory" processes stall in the tokenism band — citizens are informed or consulted, but have no real influence over the outcome.

This maps almost exactly onto Foroom's existing role hierarchy:

| Arnstein rung (band) | Foroom equivalent risk if role is under-designed |
|---|---|
| Informing | Citizen role — if citizens can only observe, this is where the whole app could stall |
| Consultation | Participant role — commenting/voting without guaranteed response is still tokenism unless proposals demonstrably feed a real decision loop |
| Partnership / delegated power | Builder role, and Council-layer note/poll placement with visible admin response |
| Citizen control | Only reached if Admin decisions are *bound* by Foroom outcomes in some auditable way (e.g., a public log of accepted/rejected proposals with reasons) |

The key design implication: **Foroom needs a visible "what happened to my proposal" loop.** Without it, the platform reproduces the exact failure mode Arnstein identified in 1960s U.S. urban renewal — extensive citizen activity that changes nothing. This is a solvable UX/governance problem (status tags on proposals: submitted → under review → accepted/rejected with reason → implemented), not a technical one, but it needs to be treated as a first-class feature, not an afterthought.

### 2.2 Communicative & collaborative planning theory (Habermas, Healey, Forester, Innes)

Where Arnstein asks "how much power do citizens have," communicative planning theory (rooted in Habermas's *Theory of Communicative Action*, developed for planning by Patsy Healey, John Forester, and Judith Innes) asks "what makes a planning *conversation* legitimate." Habermas's four conditions for an "ideal speech situation" — sincerity, legitimacy, truthfulness, comprehensibility — became the normative backbone for collaborative planning: planners act as facilitators of open debate among diverse knowledges rather than as technocrats delivering pre-formed plans, with the goal of mutual understanding rather than simply winning an argument.

Judith Innes and David Booher extended this into practice-oriented **consensus-building** methods, directly relevant to Foroom's Council-layer note/poll system and to how an AI Protector should behave: not as an arbiter that declares a winner, but as a facilitator that surfaces shared ground and makes disagreement legible (this connects directly to Pillar III below).

Known critique to keep in view: collaborative planning theory has a documented "dark side" — powerful actors can dominate even open, well-facilitated processes, and consensus-seeking can silence dissent by pressuring outliers to conform. **This is a direct argument for building minority-opinion preservation into any AI aggregation layer**, not just consensus scores (see §4.3).

> **What to build:** (1) A visible proposal lifecycle/status system so Council-layer contributions are never a black box. (2) Facilitator-mode language for the AI Protector modeled on Habermas's four sincerity/legitimacy/truthfulness/comprehensibility criteria rather than a generic "helpful assistant" persona. (3) An explicit design rule: never collapse Council discussion into a single "winning" statement without also surfacing dissenting clusters (see Pol.is/Habermas Machine below).

## 3. Pillar II — Digital twins, urban complexity, and the limits of "accurate simulation"

### 3.1 What an urban digital twin actually is (and isn't)

The current academic consensus (Batty, Bettencourt, and collaborators writing in *Nature Computational Science*) treats urban digital twins as a spectrum, from aggregate economic/behavioral models to disaggregate agent-based, cellular, and micro-simulations — not a single well-defined artifact. Reviews of citizen-centric UDTs converge on three core technical components: 3D city models, sensor/data networks, and AI-driven analytics, integrated to simulate, monitor, and enhance urban systems in near-real time.

Two cautions from the literature matter a lot for Foroom's "Living Digital Twin" framing:

- **Complexity theorists (Batty, Caldarelli et al.) argue cities are not predictable in the way engineered systems are** — not because of missing data, but because urban systems are intrinsically emergent, path-dependent, and full of feedback loops between social and physical layers. A digital twin of a city should therefore be framed as a *model for exploring scenarios and surfacing tradeoffs*, not a forecasting oracle. Overclaiming precision is both a credibility risk and, per recent philosophical critiques, an epistemological error.
- **The social/community dimension is usually the missing piece.** Reviews explicitly call out that most urban digital twins under-represent human behavior and social interaction, and argue that surveys, social media, public forums, and agent-based modeling of social dynamics need to be integrated to make a digital twin genuinely reflect urban life rather than just geometry. **This is precisely what Foroom's chat, notes, polls, and glow-heatmap are positioned to supply that most digital twin projects lack** — real, geolocated, attributable citizen sentiment data tied to 3D space in real time.

A directly relevant precedent: the *Civic Digital Twins* project for Bologna explicitly frames citizen co-design as a core layer of an urban digital twin rather than a bolt-on, which validates Foroom's basic thesis that the "digital twin" and the "participation tool" should be the same artifact, not two separate systems bridged by an export/import pipeline.

### 3.2 Standards and interoperability

CityGML (the OGC standard for semantic 3D city models) and its relationship to procedurally-generated OSM-derived geometry is the dominant interoperability layer in this space. It's worth treating Foroom's voxel format as a *rendering/interaction layer* that can, in principle, export to or ingest from CityGML/3D Tiles later, rather than a dead-end proprietary format — this matters for credibility with municipal partners and for the "open source, digital twin" framing in the original brief.

### 3.3 Directly relevant open-source precedent: VoxCity

**VoxCity** (2025) is an open-source Python framework that does almost exactly what Foroom's ingestion pipeline needs: it automatically downloads building heights, tree canopy, land cover, and terrain elevation for an arbitrary area and voxelizes buildings, trees, land cover, and terrain into an integrated grid-based 3D city model, with demonstrated global coverage (Singapore, Tokyo, Paris, NYC, Rio, Abu Dhabi, Sydney, Cape Town). It also supports simulation outputs (solar irradiance, sky/view/landmark visibility via ray tracing) aggregated onto road networks via OSMnx.

This is the single most relevant existing open-source project to study/fork/reference for Foroom's OSM→voxel pipeline — it solves the exact "least data, live voxel world from OSM" problem the brief describes, including handling multi-footprint buildings and top/bottom height data (relevant to your "buildings stacked as voxels from floorplan outline" requirement).

> **What to build:** (1) Base the voxelization pipeline on the VoxCity approach (grid generation → building/tree/terrain integration → texture/semantic tagging) rather than inventing extrusion logic from scratch. (2) Explicitly market Foroom's differentiator as *social-layer digital twin* — the missing piece complexity researchers say most UDTs lack. (3) In all public-facing copy, avoid "predictive" or "accurate forecast" language about the Simulation layer; frame it as scenario exploration, consistent with the complexity-science consensus, to avoid overclaiming.

## 4. Pillar III — Deliberative technology & computational social choice (the scientific core of the AI Protector)

This is the pillar that turns "AI Protector" from a vague concept into something with real mathematical grounding, and it is arguably the most important section for making Foroom scientifically defensible rather than gimmicky.

### 4.1 Precedents: Pol.is / vTaiwan

**Pol.is**, the open-source engine behind Taiwan's **vTaiwan** process, works by having participants post short statements and vote agree/disagree/pass on others' statements, then uses machine-learning clustering to map the resulting high-dimensional opinion space, surfacing opinion clusters and — critically — **consensus statements**: statements that draw agreement across otherwise-opposed clusters. vTaiwan has run over a decade, engaged 200,000+ participants across 26 pieces of digital-economy legislation, with roughly 80% leading to government action, and is credited with structuring the notoriously contentious Uber-regulation debate into a workable "rough consensus." Pol.is is explicitly described in the literature as having "gamified" consensus-finding, and its clustering approach avoids amplifying the loudest or most divisive voices by design (divisive/inflammatory statements simply don't cluster toward consensus and get less visual prominence).

**Decidim** (Barcelona and dozens of other cities) is the leading open-source *participatory process* platform (proposals, budgeting, voting, assemblies) — worth studying for governance/workflow structure even though it currently has little native AI integration, which is itself an opportunity: Foroom's AI Protector plus 3D spatial layer would put it ahead of the current state of the art in civic tech.

### 4.2 The AI mediator precedent: DeepMind's "Habermas Machine"

Published in *Science* (2024), DeepMind's **Habermas Machine** is the most rigorous existing precedent for an "AI Protector"-style system. It works as a **caucus mediator**: it collects individual opinions and agreement levels on a topic, generates candidate group statements, predicts how each participant would rank them, and iteratively refines statements using participants' critiques — explicitly trained to *listen and synthesize* rather than persuade. In controlled studies with over 5,000 UK participants, AI-mediated groups converged toward more common ground than groups debating directly without mediation, and participants rated the AI's group statements as clearer, more informative, and less biased than human-facilitator statements — including a replication in a demographically representative "virtual citizens' assembly" setting. Text-embedding analysis showed successful statements deliberately incorporated dissenting voices while still reflecting the majority position, rather than just averaging opinions or crowning a winner.

This is the direct model for how Foroom's AI Protector should behave in **Council-mode discussions and Simulation-mode narration**: not "vote counting," but iterative statement synthesis that is explicitly checked against minority-opinion preservation. Notably, one methodological finding to reuse: after processing critiques, the Habermas Machine tended to *over-weight* minority viewpoints — a useful calibration data point if you build a similar critique-refinement loop.

Caveat worth building into your ethics/design doc: philosophical critiques of the Habermas Machine argue that optimizing directly for "consensus" can be in tension with Habermas's own theory, in which consensus is a *byproduct* of genuine discourse, not the objective function itself. Practical implication: the AI Protector's objective should be *surfacing understanding and structuring disagreement*, with consensus statements as one output among several (clusters, dissent summaries, unresolved-tension flags) — not the only output.

### 4.3 Aggregating proposals and votes: participatory budgeting / computational social choice

Once Foroom has proposals (a bench here, a bike lane there, a demolished vs. rebuilt block in Playground) with community support, you need a defensible way to rank/select them — this is a mature subfield (participatory budgeting algorithms, computational social choice) that already faced these exact tradeoffs:

- **Knapsack voting** — voters allocate a fixed budget across proposals whose costs vary, directly modeling "which of these capital projects do we fund" — a natural fit if Foroom ever ties proposals to real municipal budgets.
- **Quadratic voting (QV)** — cost of votes grows quadratically, letting participants express *intensity* of preference (a small number of strongly-felt votes vs. many weakly-felt ones) rather than one-person-one-statement; documented tradeoffs include better minority expression but added UI complexity.
- **Proportional/representative selection rules** (Proportional Approval Voting, Expanding Approvals Rule) — designed specifically so that a numerical minority of participants with a shared strong preference still gets guaranteed representation in the outcome set, addressing the "majority tyranny" failure mode of simple upvote counts.
- **Multi-agent reinforcement learning for consensus-seeking participatory budgeting** — recent research (Majumdar & Pournaras, using real Polish PB data) shows iterative, AI-assisted negotiation between simulated voters can reach genuine consensus, not just an aggregation formula, and is comparable in fairness to standard voting-aggregation methods.

> **What to build:** (1) In Council mode, implement a Pol.is-style statement/agree-disagree/cluster visualization for proposals rather than simple upvote counts — this is a proven, well-documented mechanism and directly produces your "glow heatmap" data (statement density + cluster convergence = visual intensity). (2) For any feature tying Foroom proposals to real capital allocation, use knapsack or proportional-approval-style selection rather than plain majority vote, to avoid known fairness failure modes. (3) Build the AI Protector's core loop on the Habermas Machine's caucus-mediator pattern: collect opinions → generate candidate synthesis → predict endorsement → refine on critique → always output a minority-view summary alongside any consensus statement. (4) Rate-limit and "rhythm" the AI Protector's activity as the brief specifies — this is also supported by moderation research showing over-active AI intervention degrades trust; a scheduled/triggered cadence (e.g., respond to threshold events, not every message) is both a performance optimization and a legitimacy design choice.

## 5. Pillar IV — Representing the city: procedural generation, space syntax, and legibility

### 5.1 Procedural OSM → voxel generation

Beyond VoxCity (§3.3), the broader procedural-city-generation literature (CityEngine-style rule-based extrusion, automatic 3D building generation from OSM + LiDAR, and newer text/asset-driven approaches like CityGenAgent) converges on a consistent pipeline: (1) fetch vector footprints + height/level attributes from OSM, (2) reconstruct or fetch terrain, (3) extrude/voxelize footprints with rule-based or ML-assisted facade/roof detail, (4) apply semantic texturing (roads = impervious/gray, greenery = vegetation classes, water, etc.). This validates the brief's proposed pipeline (2D grid → stacked voxels → texture matching by OSM tag) as the standard, proven approach rather than something exotic — the main engineering risk is not the algorithm but performance at scale (LOD strategy, chunk streaming, and bounding-area limits per Foroom, which the brief already flags as necessary).

### 5.2 Space syntax — making the voxel world *legible*, not just geometrically accurate

Bill Hillier's **space syntax** theory (from *The Social Logic of Space*, 1984) offers a mathematically grounded way to reason about which streets/spaces in a Foroom's voxel world will actually feel walkable, sociable, or dead — independent of whether the geometry is photorealistic. Core tools: **axial lines** (longest sightlines through public space, forming a network whose "integration" score predicts pedestrian movement), **convex spaces** (rooms/plazas where social interaction concentrates), and **isovists** (the visible area from a given point — directly computable from a voxel grid via ray casting, which VoxCity already demonstrates for solar/view analysis). Space syntax explicitly claims a measurable, empirically testable link between spatial configuration and social/movement patterns, and has been used for decades to evaluate "before/after" effects of urban redesign — exactly the comparison Foroom's Council vs. Playground layers are structurally set up to produce.

> **What to build:** Add an optional "legibility/connectivity" analysis pass (axial-line + isovist computation over the voxel grid) that can score Playground redesigns against the existing Council-mode baseline — this gives citizens and planners an objective, literature-grounded metric ("this proposed layout increases street integration by X%") rather than only aesthetic/subjective judgment, and doubles as content for the Simulation layer.

### 5.3 Precedent: Block by Block (UN-Habitat × Mojang/Microsoft)

This is the closest real-world precedent to Foroom's entire premise, and it is extensively documented. UN-Habitat's **Block by Block** methodology has used Minecraft as a participatory design tool since 2012, integrated into its Global Public Space Programme, and has run in dozens of countries — recent figures put it at over 55 countries, 3+ million people impacted, and roughly 31,000 direct workshop participants including women, youth, elders, refugees, and people with disabilities. The documented workflow: build a base Minecraft model of the real site from maps/imagery, train small teams (2–4 people) over 2–4 days to redesign the space in-game, then present the redesign to officials/planners for costing and professional implementation. UN-Habitat's own writeup frames the mechanism explicitly: providing a low-skill-barrier ICT tool measurably increases civic engagement, especially among groups usually excluded from planning processes, precisely because Minecraft removes the need for architectural/drafting literacy.

This validates several of Foroom's specific choices (voxel/Minecraft aesthetic as an *inclusion* mechanism, not just a stylistic one; small-team collaborative building; presentation of outputs to real decision-makers) and also flags the missing piece Block by Block *doesn't* solve, which Foroom is positioned to add: Block by Block is workshop-based and episodic; Foroom's persistent, always-on, shareable-link model turns this into continuous infrastructure rather than a one-off event.

> **What to build:** Explicitly reference Block by Block's 6-stage cycle (plan → design → develop → operate → monitor → evaluate) as the backbone of a Foroom "project lifecycle" feature layered on top of an individual Foroom — this gives Council/Playground/Simulation a clear temporal narrative (a Foroom isn't just a persistent space, it's tracking a real public-space project through these stages) and gives you a UN-Habitat-aligned SDG 11 framing for grant/partnership conversations.

## 6. Pillar V — Motivation science and gamification (why people will actually keep participating)

### 6.1 Self-Determination Theory (SDT)

Ryan & Deci's SDT is the dominant motivational framework in serious-game and civic-tech design: intrinsic, sustained engagement depends on satisfying three basic psychological needs — **autonomy** (choices feel self-endorsed, not coerced), **competence** (a believable sense of mastery/progress), and **relatedness** (feeling connected to other players/community). The gamification literature draws a sharp, well-evidenced distinction between "controlled-motivation" gamification (points/badges/leaderboards used as external levers — "do X to earn Y") and "autonomous-motivation" gamification (mechanics that genuinely deliver competence feedback, meaningful choice, and social connection). The former produces short-term compliance but risks undermining intrinsic interest over time and, in some studied contexts (community-health-worker training), was perceived as surveillance rather than motivation when framed competitively.

This is directly actionable for Foroom's design:

- **Autonomy**: the limited-block Playground system, free camera switching, and open building/demolition already satisfy autonomy well — protect this; don't over-gate it with excessive permission friction.
- **Competence**: role progression (Citizen → Participant → Builder) is a natural competence ladder, but it should be *earned through legible, transparent criteria* (not opaque admin discretion) to avoid it reading as arbitrary gatekeeping.
- **Relatedness**: chat, shared Council-mode notes, and the glow-heatmap of collective activity are strong relatedness mechanics — visible collective effort is one of the most reliable relatedness cues in the SDT literature.

> **What to build:** Avoid leaderboards/competitive ranking of individual citizens' contribution counts (a controlled-motivation pattern flagged as producing anxiety/surveillance perception in analogous civic/professional contexts); prefer *collective* visible progress metrics (Foroom-level activity heatmap, project-stage progress) over individual competitive ones, especially in the Council layer where trust and legitimacy matter most.

## 7. Pillar VI — Simulation methods for the "Simulation" layer

### 7.1 Cellular automata and agent-based modeling (ABM)

Urban-growth simulation research has converged on hybrid **cellular-automata + agent-based** models: CA (grid-cell states evolving via local neighborhood rules — the classic engine being **SLEUTH**) handles the physical/environmental substrate well but cannot represent human social relations or decision-making, while ABM (individual autonomous agents — households, developers, institutions — implemented in platforms like **NetLogo**, MASON, or Repast) captures behavioral heterogeneity but is harder to calibrate at scale. Recent research explicitly combines both (e.g., informal-urbanization modeling in Accra, Ghana, combining ABM+CA and validating against SLEUTH) and — importantly for Foroom — recent reviews note that *most urban growth models still fail to meaningfully incorporate stakeholder perspectives*, treating participatory input as an add-on rather than a core input to the simulation loop.

This is exactly the gap Foroom's Simulation layer can fill differently from prior work: rather than a purely exogenous growth model, Foroom's Simulation layer can be parameterized *directly* by real Council/Playground activity (proposals, votes, note density) as agent behavior inputs, plus external open data (weather, mobility, etc.) as environmental drivers — a genuinely participatory ABM rather than a participatory-input-as-afterthought model.

> **What to build:** Architect the Simulation layer as a lightweight ABM/CA hybrid (grid = voxel Council state; agents = citizen proposal/vote activity + external data feeds as forcing functions) rather than a black-box "AI generates a future city" feature — this is both more scientifically grounded and more explainable to users and municipal partners than an opaque generative approach.

## 8. Cross-cutting risks documented in the literature (build these into governance, not just code)

1. **Tokenism creep** (Arnstein): without a visible decision-loop and transparent admin criteria, Foroom risks becoming an elaborate consultation theater regardless of how good the 3D tooling is.
2. **Consensus-washing / minority erasure** (Habermas Machine critiques, collaborative-planning "dark side" literature): any AI synthesis feature must structurally preserve and surface dissent, not just produce one "winning" statement.
3. **Digital-twin overclaiming** (Batty, complexity-science critiques): market/label the Simulation layer as scenario exploration, not prediction.
4. **Controlled-motivation gamification backfire** (SDT literature): avoid individual competitive leaderboards, especially in governance-adjacent (Council) contexts.
5. **Digital divide / accessibility**: Block by Block's own literature stresses that ICT participation tools only increase engagement when accessibility barriers (skill, hardware, connectivity) are actively addressed — the brief's "join via link, no login, works on mobile" design is the right instinct; keep protecting it as new features are added, since it is the single biggest lever for reaching the groups traditional planning processes exclude (per Arnstein's original critique).
6. **Voting-mechanism fairness**: plain upvote/majority counts are documented to under-represent minorities in participatory budgeting; prefer proportional/knapsack-style mechanisms wherever proposals compete for limited resources or attention.
